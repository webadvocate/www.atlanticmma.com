Exclusive Interview with Hall of Famer Mark “The Hammer” Coleman

Posted in MMA Blog

LOS ANGELES — When asked what the fondest memory of his nearly 13-year mixed martial arts career is, Ultimate Fighting Championship Hall of Famer Mark “The Hammer” Coleman replies with silence. It’s almost as if his entire career is flashing before him.

Regarded as the pioneer of ground-and-pound fighting, he won UFC X and XI tournaments, was the organization’s first-ever heavyweight titlist and stunned the fighting world when he claimed victory at the inaugural PRIDE Fighting Championships Grand Prix. He’s fought the best then and now, from Don Frye and Dan Severn to Mauricio “Shogun” Rua and Fedor Emelianenko, building a career resume worthy of warranting a spot in the UFC Hall of Fame, as he’s one of just five fighters to do so.

When the silence ends, the 44-year-old is still pondering. His debut at UFC X saw him pound out three victories in one evening, a dominating first appearance that launched a brilliant career. “I expected to win that thing for sure, but to follow through and do it is another story,” Coleman says.

A completely different story was his crowning four years later at the 2000 PRIDE Grand Prix, as he entered the tournament on a four-fight losing streak and at a career crossroads. Amidst a star-studded field of 16 combatants that featured mixed martial arts luminaries such as Royce Gracie, Kaz Sakuraba, Mark Kerr and Kaz Fujita, among others, Coleman defeated Igor Vovchanchyn in the finals, defeating the seemingly unstoppable “Ice Cold,” who was unbeaten in his previous 32 fights. “PRIDE was special because I did have to come back from four straight losses,” Coleman says. “I was extremely motivated by all the people who counted me out.”

That was then, however. “That’s 10 years ago,” he states emphatically.

And for the first time in more than two years, as it relates to MMA, Coleman is all about what’s next. He returns to where it all began on Saturday, as he makes his way back to the octagon for the co-headlining bout of UFC 93, taking on Mauricio “Shogun” Rua in a rematch of a 2006 fight won by Coleman in PRIDE. It’s a bout that features Coleman’s return to the UFC for the first time since January of 1999, his debut in the 205-pound light-heavyweight division and a lot of bad blood.

“We have a little history,” says Coleman, who defeated Rua in just 49 seconds after the latter posted wrong and gruesomely injured his arm after a Coleman takedown. “Him making this fight personal, I guess that’s forcing me to make it personal.”

Coleman’s Hammerhouse camp and Rua’s previous camp, Chute Box, engaged in an infamous melee after the bout and the nearly three years between fights have done nothing to quell the friction.

“It just makes it that much better,” UFC commentator Mike Goldberg says. “It makes the story better.”

For Goldberg, an Ohio native like Coleman, seeing the Hall of Famer return to the UFC is quite a homecoming.

“It’s an absolute honor to have the Hammer coming back,” Goldberg says. “He’s a credit to the sport. … He’s an example of that athlete who was way better than anyone at one thing.”

But, as Goldberg would add, Coleman realizes how much the sport has changed as well, something “The Hammer” is quick to touch upon, himself.

“The difference now is guys back in the day had their one-dimensional style,” says Coleman, who was an NCAA champion at Ohio State before becoming a member of the 1992 U.S. Olympic wrestling team. “It went in waves, first it was jiu jitsu, then it was wrestling.”

“Now, if you don’t have skills in all three areas [jiu jitsu, striking and wrestling], you don’t stand a chance.”

There are many who don’t believe Coleman stands a chance. Whether it’s age, the fact that many still color him as a one-dimensional fighter or the fact that the highly regarded Rua’s loss was seen by many as a fluke, “Shogun” is the heavy favorite.

But Coleman’s fine with that. He’s likely one of his harshest critics. Known for his unbridled post-fight passion, whether it’s a crazy celebration after a victory or a profanity-laced diatribe after a loss, he wears his emotions on his sleeve much of the time, but is also a surprisingly easy going individual more often than not.

“It’s been said I have no idea how to control my emotions, I guess I have to agree,” he says. “I’m actually pretty laid back most of the time, I’m fairly unorganized. I train hard when the time comes, but lack of consistency training hard is probably my biggest regret.”

Those regrets have led to Coleman’s biggest weaknesses – a reliance on his wrestling style and a lack of stamina. But for this fight, he says his training has been at its best. And, as any fighter of his age is accustomed to, as he prepares for it, he’s inundated with questions as to why he continues to fight. For him, there really isn’t even a question, though.

“It’s all I’ve every really done. Nobody really wants to give up something they love,” Coleman says. “It’s not a question of why, it’s what I do.”

And as monumental as his past triumphs have been, what lies ahead is bigger than anything that came before.

“The only one that matters now is this one,” he says. “This one is incredibly huge, this is obviously the most important fight of my life.”

Bringing Back The "Art" In The "Martial"

Posted in MMA Blog

When I was growing up I heard that no one who studied martial arts long enough could be evil, that through years of discipline and hard training, like the process of purifying steel, all their impurities would be burnt away. When I saw “Karate Kid” I was like, “Sure that guy’s evil—but it’s a movie! That doesn’t happen in real life.” It wasn’t until competing in mixed martial arts and reading commentary by supposed “martial artists” on the Internet that I realized that one can study for years and years and still be a prick.

Let’s break down the two words “martial,” as in of, related to, or suited for war or a warrior, which disqualifies the aerobic kickboxing class attendant who is only focusing on the exercise aspect of training benefit and forgetting on what the heck the whole thing is based. The second part of the term is “art,” as in the conscious use of skill and creative imagination especially in the production of aesthetic objects; something of beauty. If your whole gig is just about “kicking some ass, yo!” then you are not an artist, you are a sociopath. And if you’re whole schtick is about fancy costumes and triple back somersaults with a bendable sword in your hand, you may be a beautiful artist, but you ain’t no “martialist.”

Too many “artists” have found themselves in their first MMA competition lying on their backs looking up at a referee counting them out as they question, “Mommy, is it time for hot cocoa yet?” And while a strict “martialist” may fair better in the ring, they tend to lack the beauty of movement and expression; street fights may be fun to watch on YouTube but in the ring it seems to lack something: the “artistry.”

At competitions that had both fighting and forms competitions, I’ve talked to many “tai chi” practitioners who deceive themselves that “If I were attacked my body would just naturally move how it needs to defend myself.” Bullshit. You have to train up to speed if you are planning on using any technique martially. Forgetting the chuan (“fist”) in tai chi chuan, the only hope of defeating an attacker with it is to move so slowly that your attacker wets himself with laughter allowing you to outrun the soggy-panted assailant. I have seen just as many “yogis” who have mastered the art of sitting on the meditation cushion or have memorized 600 chants, yet “in the ring” if someone does not have the aroma of incense and OMs, they don’t know how to deal.

A big wake-up call came when The Ultimate Fighting Competition first started. We saw many martial artists that appeared more “artist” than “martial.” As these contests progressed, we saw the disappearance of the wearing of a traditional gi in competition and we also saw no one stepping into the ring without a modicum of grappling work under his belt.

Put together, “martial artist,” and you have a beautiful expression of a skilled, disciplined fighting marvel that can garner respect and appreciation from anyone who is graced enough to see him in action, spanning the whole human dictionary from the Asshole to the Zealot. Yes, at times it may look brutal as to what the MMA competitor may be required to do, but one who can see the kung fu, or “skilled man” (or woman) in the brutality is the one who can see the art in the martial. And like the older couple who were staring at “The Kramer” portrait in that “Seinfeld” episode, you will find, “He is a loathsome, offensive brute…yet I can’t look away.”

So if you want to call yourself a “martial artist,” do what you need to do in the ring—tap into your inner animal, bang your head against the ropes or cage to psych yourself up, take out your opponent in whatever way your training and instincts provide—but stop acting like idiots on the Internet and stop acting like thugs in and out of the competition arena.

There is a metaphysical law that says, “Whatever you want to be given to you, you have to give to others.” If you want love, you have to give love. If you want respect, you have to give respect. And if you act like a jackass, you attract more of that nonsense to yourself and you force true martial artists to be associated with you and look like jackasses as well.

One of my New-Age friends fast-forwards any violent scenes in movies because she thinks that a physical expression of violence is somehow “not of the light.” Perhaps it is just her eyes that need to be adjusted so that she can see the artist in everyone. So while she avoided watching V’s final fight scene in “V for Vendetta,” I was glued to the set, misty-eyed. It wasn’t because I‘m a pussy. It was because it was the perfect expression of a man in his flow and the message that when one is in his flow nothing can stop him from accomplishing what he came here to do.

That is “religion”: to connect with your flow, your “God,” and to live authentically. That is how we make the world a better place. It doesn’t matter whether you are working towards world peace, feeding the hungry or fighting in a ring—it is about being in your flow and thus expressing your own unique expression of “God.”

But for the most part, organized religion is just a group of people who believe in the same fairy tale and feel that by gathering together periodically and waxing superior to everyone who believes in a different myth that they have some self-worth. “My God could kick your God’s ass!” is rephrased in intellectual philosophic terms in an attempt to mask the non-loving message they are preaching to anyone who “trains in a different camp.” They seem to “fast-forward” over anyone that doesn’t fit into their “ideal” movie in the form of insult, ridicule, hate and murder. More people have been killed in the name of religion than in any MMA ring and yet we have many more people calling for the ban of extreme fighting sports than religion. Sounds a little backwards to me.

Rather than trying to isolate and package our form of God (or martial art) so that we can market it to the masses and have more people join our cult of separation, maybe we can let God out of the bottle and realize that he includes everything, from the cloud to the tree to the human to the rock, from the scent of a flower to the feeling of ecstasy—to two people matching their martial skill in a ring or stage or octagon with limited gear and limited rules.

Our goal as martial artists should be to grow not only as martial artists but as people. If we gain some titles and sponsorships along the way it’s gravy. If we gain many battle wounds along the way, hopefully they came with life-learning lessons and not just unconsciousness. If we gain some friends and mutual respect on our path, then we’ve gained something more valuable than a belt or trophy.

When Dana White took over the UFC, he made the organization and MMA gain a lot of respect as a “real” sport. It is up to the martial arts community to behave respectfully and hopefully more will start to have the “eyes to see and the ears to hear” the artistry in the martial and realize everyone is your “training brother” in the octagon of life.

REFLECTION:

When you look at another martial artist (or style), do you immediately judge what is “lacking” or do you appreciate the skills they have trained and attained? If you are going to fight them, you need to look at both, their strengths to be careful of and their vulnerabilities in which to exploit.

In life, when you look at people, do you immediately judge them for what you see as their deficiencies, such as looks, clothes, money, education, or do you see them as a reflection of where you may need to put in some more work. For instance, if someone irritates the heck out of you, you can think, “That guy’s a jerk!” or you can explore what vulnerabilities (buttons) that you have inside that are not serving you to give your best fight (reaction.)
